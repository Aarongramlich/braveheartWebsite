California Consumer Privacy Act


The California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) is a bill that enhances privacy rights and consumer protection for residents in the state of California. The bill was passed on June 28, 2018 and becomes effective on January 1, 2020.

New legislation has created a more enforceable framework for businesses, by:


  • Creates a more structured understanding of "personal information".
  • Empowers California consumers with new data privacy rights, including:
    1. Right to know
    2. Right to access
    3. Right to delete
    4. Right to opt-out of the sale of data
  • Requiring designated methods for submitting data request, including at mininum a toll-free telephone number, and at least one other channel defined by individual businesses.
  • Enforces processes be in place to obtain consent on the data sharing of minors.
  • Automatic fines up to $7,500 for each intentional violation and $2,500 for each unintentional violation.

There are some exceptions to the law. CCPA will not restrict your business from:


  • Complying with federal, state, or local laws
  • Complying with civil, criminal, or regulatory inquiries and investigations
  • Collecting, using, retaining, selling, or disclosing consumer information that is de-identified or aggregate. Businesses must not attempt to re-identify these consumers.

CCPA applies to organizations that conduct business in California, and stafisfy one of the following three conditions:


  1. Gross revenue in excess of $25 million
  2. Annually buys, receives, sells, or shares the personal information of 50,000 or more conumers, households, or devices (individually or in combination)
  3. Derives 50% or more of it's annual revenue from the sale of consumer personal information

Organizations that meet CCPA criteria need to prepare for compliance.


  1. Establish and maintain a data inventory of personal information collected or sold from California residents.
  2. Revise and update privacy notices.
  3. Verifiable consumer requests.
  4. Access rights.
  5. Erasure rights.
  6. Right to opt-out of sale of personal information.
  7. Update service level agreements with 3rd party data processers.
  8. Train personnel with access to personal information about CCPA requirements.
